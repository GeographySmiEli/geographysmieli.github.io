Forest Fire Hazard Risk within the San Bernardino National Forest's Mountaintop Ranger District

Analysis

In my research on forest fire hazard risk within the San Bernardino National Forest's Mountaintop Ranger District, I've employed multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) techniques. My focus was on evaluating topography, human infrastructure proximity, and vegetation density derived from satellite imagery as essential criteria.

In the first step of my study, I aimed to determine the suitable decision rule for MCDA. Through tests like Moran's I, I discovered significant spatial autocorrelation among criteria, leading to the conclusion that the Simple Additive Weighting (SAW) rule wasn't suitable due to this high autocorrelation. Consequently, I proposed TOPSIS as the more fitting decision rule. I delved into various weighting methodologies (subjective, objective, and combined) to assess criteria. I found that the choice of weighting method—be it subjective or combined—had minimal impact on fire risk assessment outcomes when employing TOPSIS for decision-making.
